Who Gets to go Home?

The Story Behind the Project

Why: While my grandmother survived her bout with COVID-19, navigating her post-discharge care revealed how fragile the support system is for families lacking medical networks. Left to manage complex recovery plans entirely on our own, I realized that discharge from a hospital is often where patient care completely breaks down. I built this project to see if broader local data validated what our family experienced firsthand.

The Missing Data: To map these gaps across Charlotte-Mecklenburg, I began analyzing federal readmission tracking metrics. However, I uncovered a major systemic blind spot: Medicare (CMS) explicitly suspended hospital readmission penalties and reporting for the first half of 2020. This policy design choice shielded hospital systems from immense financial liability during the initial pandemic surge, but it inadvertently erased the critical public data trail required to evaluate patient outcomes during a historic healthcare crisis.

The Road to Understanding: Because real patient charts are restricted under strict HIPAA privacy laws and the historical 2020 data was effectively missing, I had to pivot. Rather than walk away from the analysis, I engineered a data simulation engine that modeled 6,845 detailed, individual patient clinical trajectories. To ensure these synthetic records mirrored real-world dynamics, I mathematically anchored the baseline distributions directly to the actual **FY 2026 CMS Hospital Performance Registry** and local **DHHS regional health datasets**.

The Goal: Generating this synthetic framework did more than circumvent privacy limitations—it allowed me to build an end-to-end data engineering pipeline. With this structured environment, I was able to inject intentional missingness to test cleaning protocols, isolate hidden socio-economic and demographic disparities, and construct a functional, interactive dashboard designed to show hospital operations teams exactly where post-discharge care protocols require intervention.

Operational Impact & Policy Recommendations

A translation of data-driven findings into strategic hospital interventions designed to reduce CMS readmission penalty exposures.

Finding 1: Operational Discharge Drift

Equity-Conditioned Discharge

Patients living in under-resourced zip codes show lower average lengths of stay than the 3.04-day standard system baseline, resulting in premature discharge before clinical stability is fully reached. Policy Implementation: Mandate standardized clinical stabilization windows for high-risk chronic conditions (e.g., heart failure) that explicitly adjust based on the patient's available home care infrastructure.

Finding 2: Care Loop Deficits

Warm Hand-off Navigators

Minority cohorts such as Black and Latino communities experience a sharp 58-59% gap in completing critical post-care discharge, compared to Asian patients at 31% and white patients at 32%. Policy Implementation: Deploy hospital-funded bilingual Patient Navigators tasked with legally scheduling and securing transportation for a primary care check-in within 7 days of discharge.

Finding 3: Regional Pharmacy Deserts

Transitional Courier Care

Readmission spike within Charlotte's historic "Crescent" corridor, driven by systemic pharmaceutical deserts and lack of immediate access to medication. Policy Implementation: Establish partnerships supplying 30 days of free, hospital-couriered prescription delivery directly to high-risk residential zip codes.
