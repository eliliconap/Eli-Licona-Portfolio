Who Gets to go Home?

The Story Behind the Project

Why I Built This: This project started because of something personal. During the peak of COVID-19, I watched how messy and broken the healthcare system became for families who lacked resources, including my own grandmother. When patients were sent home from the hospital, there was barely any support. This left families to figure out complicated medical care completely on their own, and I wanted to see if the data backed up what we went through.

The Missing Data Problem: I started digging into Charlotte-Mecklenburg hospital readmission data to track these care gaps, but I immediately hit a major roadblock. It turns out that the federal government, through CMS, skipped tracking readmission numbers for the first half of 2020. They did this so hospitals wouldn't get hit with massive financial penalties while dealing with the chaos of the pandemic. While that protected the hospitals financially, it completely erased the data needed to track how vulnerable families were being left behind.

The road to understanding: Real patient data is locked down tight under HIPAA laws, and with the 2020 data erased, I had to get creative. Instead of quitting, I decided to build the data myself. I engineered a realistic dataset simulating 6,845 individual patient journeys. To make sure the numbers actually made sense in the real world, I anchored my simulated patients directly to real baselines: the "FY 2026 CMS Hospital Performance Metrics" and the local "DHHS Public Health Dataset".

The Goal: Going this route didn't just let me bypass privacy laws, it allowed me to build a much stronger project. This dataset gave me a perfect sandbox to clean up messy data, look for hidden patterns, and build an interactive dashboard that clearly shows where the system breaks down so we can find ways to fix it.

Operational Impact & Policy Recommendations

A translation of data-driven findings into strategic hospital interventions designed to reduce CMS readmission penalty exposures.

Finding 1: Operational Discharge Drift

Equity-Conditioned Discharge

Patients living in under-resourced zip codes show lower average lengths of stay than the 3.04-day standard system baseline, resulting in premature discharge before clinical stability is fully reached. Policy Implementation: Mandate standardized clinical stabilization windows for high-risk chronic conditions (e.g., heart failure) that explicitly adjust based on the patient's available home care infrastructure.

Finding 2: Care Loop Deficits

Warm Hand-off Navigators

Minority cohorts display a sharp 58% breakdown rate in completing critical 30-day post-discharge clinical follow-ups. Policy Implementation: Deploy hospital-funded bilingual Patient Navigators tasked with legally scheduling and securing transportation for a primary care check-in within 7 days of discharge.

Finding 3: Regional Pharmacy Deserts

Transitional Courier Care

Readmission spikes mathematically cluster within Charlotte's historic "Crescent" corridor, driven by systemic pharmaceutical deserts and lack of immediate access to medication. Policy Implementation: Establish partnerships supplying 30 days of free, hospital-couriered prescription delivery directly to high-risk residential zip codes.
