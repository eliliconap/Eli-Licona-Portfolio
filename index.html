Who Gets to Go Home?

Charlotte-Mecklenburg Hospital Readmissions: A Clinical & Regional Equity Audit

When my grandmother survived COVID-19 in 2020, her recovery exposed a chaotic, under-resourced healthcare loop. Wanting to audit these regional care disparities across Charlotte-Mecklenburg, I hit a massive roadblock: federal CMS registries intentionally suppressed readmission data from the first half of 2020 to shield hospital margins from pandemic penalties.

Rather than abandon the audit due to this data gap and HIPAA privacy bounds, I engineered a highly realistic synthetic engine simulating 6,845 unique patient journeys anchored to real-world benchmarks from the FY 2026 CMS Registry and regional DHHS datasets. This project exposes hidden post-discharge inequities actively impacting elderly, Medicaid, and minority cohorts.

Predictive Analysis

How do road infrastructure characteristics, weather conditions, and socioeconomic factors relate to the likelihood of serious or fatal traffic crashes across Charlotte, North Carolina?

Charlotte is one of the fastest growing cities in America and its road infrastructure was not built for the population it now carries. My team and I analyzed nearly 500,000 crashes spanning 2010 to 2025 and found that crash severity is not random. Crash severity clusters geographically in predictable patterns driven by three compounding forces: how roads are designed, what the weather is doing, and how wealthy the neighborhood is.
